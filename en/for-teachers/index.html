For Teachers

  • We develop combinations of AI teaching tools, methods, and learning modules suitable for pre-primary and primary education

  • We develop pedagogical frameworks for AI and safety education

  • We promote the realization of children's rights in AI-based systems

We develop applications and materials together with teachers and students!

The project organizes a series of participatory AI-related co-design projects and practical exercises for teacher trainees and pre-primary and primary school teachers and students. In practice, the project involves 90 teachers and teacher trainees, as well as 360 school children in pre-primary and primary education.

In order to engage learners in designing and implementing their own AI and machine learning-based solutions, we use age-appropriate tools, and especially learning tools and materials developed in the project.

The goal is to help learners, teachers, and teacher trainees understand AI/machine learning (AI/ML) security issues, technologies, and models through experimentation and invention.

We develop applications that support AI education

We develop and provide easy-to-use applications that focus particularly on the interaction between artificial intelligence and cybersecurity.

These can support self-study by children and families in addition to school teaching.

We evaluate the usability and suitability of the teaching tools we develop for cybersecurity and combating disinformation, prejudice, and stereotypes.

In order to achieve the broadest possible impact, we design applications to be scalable (work on all devices) and easy to implement

GenAI Trainable Machine

Trainable Machine is an intuitive application with which you can easily implement a simple AI application yourself.

We publish guides and recommendations addressing children's rights and adult responsibilities

The project adds research on children's rights (Convention on the Rights of the Child - CRC) to the AI field. We create guides and recommendations for teachers and guardians during the project.

The research uses legal dogmatic research to problematize the legal requirements of data authorities and identify AI risks to children's rights from protection and participation perspectives. The legal framework is compared with learners' attitudes and understanding of their rights regarding their data and algorithmic manipulation.

In the second three-year period, the project will produce recommendations for governments, guardians, teacher educators, and educators (e.g., schools) on how they can fulfill their obligations regarding AI and children's rights (relating, for example, to specific AI transparency requirements, children's data management, and children's online profiling issues).

The project examines legal concepts relating to children's consent and decision-making capacity in AI-based systems. It analyzes the legislative age limits for children's consent and demonstrates parents' responsibility for giving and transferring consent to schools.

The project critically examines legal norms regarding children's consent, particularly those related to data protection regulations. It also examines the requirements for decision-making in AI-based teaching and learning tools, such as what kind of AI/ML schools can use in teaching with the child's consent, parental permission, and the school's teaching mandate.
